Fun Facts About National Parks

Toggle on/off
Fact Number One
Mount McKinley in the Alaska Range of Denali National Park is the highest point in the national parks at 20,302 feet.
Fact Number Two
The parks are also home to the lowest point in the western hemisphere: Badwater Basin in Death Valley, California, is 282 feet below sea level.
Fact Number Three
California and Alaska each have eight national parks.
Fact Number Four
Arches National Park is famous for having the largest concentration of stone arches in the world.
Fact Number Five
Everglades National Park protects more than 25% of Florida's original everglades (subtropical wetlands).
Fact Number Six
Dry Tortugas National Park in Florida is known for its world-renowned concentration of turtles.
Fact Number Seven
Alaska is home to one of the least visited national parks: Kobuk Valley National Park receives just 3,000 visitors per year.
Fact Number Eight
At 1,932 feet deep, Crater Lake National Park in Oregon is the deepest lake in the U.S.
Fact Number Nine
he smallest national park unit is part of a part of an acre in downtown Philadelphia, the Thaddeus Kosciuszko National Memorial.
Fact Number Ten
The national parks are home and habitat to more than 400 endangered or threatened plant and animal species.

Acadia National Park

  • located along the mid-section of the Maine coast
  • Acadia has a rich human history, dating back more than 10,000 years ago with the Wabanaki people.
  • The landscape architect Charles Eliot is credited with the idea for the park.

National Park of American Samoa

  • located in the United States territory of American Samoa
  • The park preserves and protects coral reefs, tropical rainforests, fruit bats, and the Samoan culture
  • Popular activities include hiking and snorkeling.

Arches National Park

  • located in eastern Utah
  • The park contains the highest density of natural arches in the world.
  • Astronomy is also popular in the park due to its dark skies, despite the increasing light pollution from towns such as Moab
